What Is Safe to Share

Learn how to judge whether a prompt, file, or pasted content is safe to use in AI tools through practical examples of safe and unsafe inputs.

How AI tools generally handle your data
  • Most tools use your input within the session to generate responses.
  • Data handling differs by tool, account type, and settings (personal vs enterprise).
  • Some tools may store or use data for service improvement unless disabled or governed by enterprise policies.
What this means for you
  • Treat public tools/personal accounts as external by default.
  • Always use company-approved or enterprise versions for work data.
  • Always assume sensitive data requires stricter controls.
What to look for
  • Safe examples: public information, generic templates, already-approved training content, or non-sensitive examples.
  • Use caution with internal documents, process details, business plans, financial figures, customer information, and meeting notes.
  • Do not paste personal data, confidential company information, customer data, or anything you would not want shared externally.
Simple rule of thumb
If you would not feel comfortable emailing it outside the company, do not paste it into an unapproved AI tool.
Examples to review
Safe: “Draft a friendly reminder email for a meeting tomorrow at 3 PM.”
Unsafe: “Summarize this confidential customer complaint and include names, order details, and internal notes.”
Borderline: “Rewrite this internal process note.” Only do this in approved tools and only when the content is allowed.
The goal is not to avoid AI. The goal is to use AI without exposing information that should stay protected.

Personal vs Work Accounts

Understand the difference between personal and work AI accounts, and why account type matters for privacy, governance, and responsible use.

Why this matters
  • Work accounts are usually managed with company controls, access policies, and approved data settings.
  • Personal accounts are not governed by the company and may not meet internal security expectations.
  • Even if a personal account feels convenient, it may create unnecessary risk when used for company work. Data from personal accounts may be stored or shared in ways that do not align with company policies.
What to check
  • Which account are you logged into right now?
  • Are you using a company-approved version of the tool?
  • Do you understand whether your inputs are stored, shared, or used beyond your immediate session?
Good practice
Use work-approved AI tools for work content. Keep personal accounts for personal experimentation with non-sensitive content only.

Generate a video-style explanation of a concept such as cash against documents, then try creating your own.

3B
10 min

Databricks Genie: Ask Data Questions

Ask business questions in natural language and explore data insights.

How it works
  • Genie understands your question in natural language.
  • It translates your question into a data query (such as SQL).
  • It retrieves results from company data and presents them as tables or summaries.
Try these questions
  • What are some most recent trades in APAC?
  • What is our position in butter?
Key idea
Genie does not generate answers from general knowledge. It pulls real answers directly from your company’s data.
What to keep in mind
  • This is a governed environment with controlled data access.
  • You still need business judgment to interpret results.
  • Always sanity check outputs before using them.
Genie reduces effort to get data, but you are still responsible for interpreting it correctly.
